# Banned phrases and AI-tells

Two layers. Layer 1 is vocabulary. Layer 2 is structure and rhythm, which is what actually makes a clean-vocabulary email still read as AI.

## Layer 1 — Kill list with replacements

Calibrated against the user's real sent mail. These signal "template," "sales sequence," or "LLM," and he does not use them. Cut or replace.

| Banned | Why it's a tell | Use instead |
|---|---|---|
| Excited to connect / thrilled to | Fake enthusiasm, networking-template | State the actual reason you're writing |
| Passionate about | Resume filler, says nothing | What you actually did or care about, concretely |
| Driven growth / unlock value / drive impact | Consultant boilerplate | The specific result, with a number if you have one |
| Synergies / synergistic | Nobody says this to a human | "where our work overlaps," or cut |
| Game-changing / revolutionary / cutting-edge | Marketing hyperbole | Describe what it does, plainly |
| Leverage (verb) | Corporate tic | "use," "draw on" |
| I came across your profile / I stumbled upon | Cold-template tell | Name the specific thing you actually saw |
| I'd love to pick your brain | Networking cliche | "I'd value your take on X" |
| Let's connect / let's sync | Vague | Propose the actual format and time |
| At the end of the day | Padding | cut |
| As per / per my last email | Passive-aggressive in replies | "as I mentioned," or just restate briefly |
| Kindly | Stiff, often non-native tell | "please," or cut |
| I hope this email finds you well | Dead robotic opener | His real warmer variants below |

## Layer 1b — NOT banned: soft connectors that are genuinely his voice

v1 of this skill over-banned these. The user's actual sent mail uses them constantly, and they read as warm and human in the registers where he uses them. Do NOT strip them in warm, networking, recruiter, or transactional contexts. Trim them only for cold outreach to senior strangers, where economy matters more.

- **"Thanks for reaching out!"** / "reaching out" - he uses this in nearly every recruiter reply. Keep it.
- **"Just following up in case my last message got buried."** - his standard, gentle nudge. Keep it.
- **"Just touching base to see if..."** / **"let me circle back in a week or two"** - real phrases in his warm follow-ups. Keep.
- **"I just wanted to check in"** / "just wanted to" - fine as a soft opener to people he knows.
- **"Hope you're doing well"** / **"Hope you are well!"** / **"I hope the year is off to a great start for you!"** - his genuine warm openers for network contacts. These are the human cousins of the banned robotic "I hope this email finds you well." Keep them for warm relationships; skip for cold.
- **"feel free to book / grab a slot"** - pairs with his Calendly link. Keep.
- **"Looking forward to connecting!"** / "Looking forward to catching up!" - his real closers. Keep; they carry genuine warmth.

The principle: he's writing warm professional-network email, not cold sales sequences. Friendly connective tissue is on-brand. Strip manufactured sales language (Layer 1), not ordinary warmth.

Context exception: a banned phrase used as a deliberate, self-aware joke (e.g. "circling back is exactly what I said I wouldn't do") can work once. Used straight, it stays banned.

## Layer 2 — Structural and rhythmic tells

These survive a clean vocabulary pass and are the real giveaways.

- **Em dashes.** The number-one AI signature. Banned outright. Hyphen with spaces, comma, or two sentences.
- **Tricolon everywhere.** AI loves "X, Y, and Z" rhythm in every sentence. Real people vary. Break the pattern.
- **The setup-then-payoff sentence.** "It's not just about A, it's about B." "This isn't merely X, it's Y." Delete on sight.
- **Symmetrical paragraph lengths.** Three tidy paragraphs of equal size reads machine-made. Let length vary; a one-line paragraph is fine and human.
- **Hedge-stacking.** "I think it might possibly be worth perhaps considering." Pick one. State it.
- **Over-signposting.** "Firstly... Secondly... In conclusion..." in a 4-sentence email. Cut all of it.
- **Empty enthusiasm toward strangers.** Exclamation marks and "Great to hear!", "Absolutely!", "Love this!" aimed at someone the user barely knows, or used to manufacture energy a cold email hasn't earned. Note the boundary: *genuine* warmth to people he knows is part of his real voice ("Большое спасибо!!", "Pleasure to e-meet you!", "talk to you tomorrow!"). Keep that. Cut only the fake, strategic, or stranger-directed enthusiasm.
- **Restating the recipient back to them.** "As someone who clearly values innovation..." They know who they are. Don't narrate them.
- **The credential dump.** Three sentences of the user's background before the ask. One relevant line, then the point.
- **Throat-clearing openers.** "I wanted to take a moment to..." "I'm writing to you today because..." Start at the point.
- **Uniform politeness padding** at both ends. One warm beat is plenty; two on each side smothers the message.

## The test

Read the draft out loud as if a busy, slightly skeptical senior peer received it. If any line would make them think "this is a template," "this person is selling me," or "a bot wrote this," cut that line. What remains should sound like one professional writing to another with something specific to say.
