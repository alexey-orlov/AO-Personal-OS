# Message playbooks

Structure and a short worked example per message type. Structures are scaffolding, not templates to fill mechanically. All examples obey the house style: no em dashes, one ask, business-casual, no AI clichés. Email examples below are shown in their **Path A** form (no signature in body — Gmail auto-appends `Best regards, / Alex Orlov, / alexorlov.co | Linkedin` on send). For **Path B** drafts (Gmail API `create_draft`), append the standing signature block from `profile.md` at the bottom. See `SKILL.md` Step 3 for which path applies.

## Outreach (cold or warm)

**Goal:** get a reply. Not a meeting booked from the first message, not admiration. A reply.

**Structure:**
1. One specific opening line tied to the recipient (their work, a mutual connection, something concrete and real, not flattery).
2. One or two sentences on who the user is and why it's relevant, framed as a peer-level exchange of experience or perspective, not a pitch.
3. One low-friction ask (a short call, a reply to a question, a pointer).

Frame it as two professionals who'd benefit from comparing notes. Don't sell. Don't stack credentials; one relevant line is enough, and the purpose must sit above it, not under it.

**Example (cold + peer, email):**
> Subject: CRM product expansion at [Company]
>
> Hi Dana,
>
> I saw [Company] is moving the platform into vertical CRM. I led that same shift at Creatio, including the part where the horizontal product team and the vertical bets start competing for the same roadmap.
>
> I'm a product exec in B2B SaaS, currently looking at agentic AI in this space. Happy to compare notes if useful, no agenda.
>
> Worth a 20-minute call, or easier over email?

## Reply inside an existing thread

**Goal:** answer what they asked, move it forward, match their register.

**Structure:** address their open question first, then anything you're adding, then the next step if there is one. Mirror their greeting/sign-off and warmth (see `register-calibration.md`). Don't re-introduce yourself or restate context they already have.

## Working-peer collaboration / meeting follow-up

**Goal:** keep a live working relationship moving - summarize what was discussed, confirm alignment, tee up next steps. This is internal/peer working mode, not favor-asking or outreach. High-frequency for him, usually in Russian/Ukrainian with the direct reports and peers he works with daily.

**Structure:** brief greeting, a structured recap of what was agreed + next steps (a short list or inline summary is correct here, unlike in cold mail), an explicit invite to confirm or correct, and any logistics (invite sent, next sync). Flag open points honestly rather than papering over them. This register is crisp and unceremonious, not warm-networky.

His real markers: `Структурую те, про що говорили на зустрічі вчора + наступні кроки (👉). Подивись, чи так само все бачиш.` / `кинув інвайт` / `Саммарі сьогоднішнього обговорення - inline...` Crisp acknowledgement of a peer's input: `Супер, дякую, прийнято!` Honest flag of an unresolved point: `По п.5 голосом ще проговоримо, бо не впевнений, що до кінця зрозумів тебе.`

**Example (UA, peer):**
> Богдан, привіт!
>
> Структурую те, про що говорили вчора + наступні кроки (нижче 👉). Подивись, чи так само все бачиш.
>
> [1-3 crisp points / inline summary]
>
> По наступних кроках можемо попрацювати знову у двох в понеділок (кинув інвайт).

## Follow-up

**Goal:** restart the thread without nagging.

**Structure depends on the relationship.**
- *Cold / value-led outreach:* don't just nag. Add a new angle, a development, or a narrower ask. The v1 "add something new" rule applies here.
- *Warm / transactional (recruiters, people who owe a reply, scheduling):* a plain, light nudge is exactly what he sends, and it's fine. No new angle required. His real template: "Hope you're doing well. Just following up in case my last message got buried. Looking forward to catching up!" Keep it short, friendly, unembarrassed. No apology spiral either way.

**Example (warm-but-distant, value-led):**
> Hi Dana,
>
> Circling back is exactly what I said I wouldn't do, so instead: we just shipped the vertical-CRM piece I mentioned, and the roadmap-conflict problem played out in a way I think maps to what you're building.
>
> Happy to walk through it if it's useful. If the timing's off, no worries at all.

**Example (warm/transactional nudge - his actual style):**
> Hi Grant,
>
> Hope you're doing well! Just following up in case my last message got buried. Looking forward to catching up!

## Network check-in / re-engagement

**Goal:** reopen a warm-but-dormant relationship (recruiters, VCs, past contacts) and ask if anything relevant has come up. This is one of his highest-frequency genres.

**Structure:** warm seasonal/personal opener, one line acknowledging it's been a while, the soft ask, an easy out. His real template:
> Hi [Name],
>
> I hope the year is off to a great start for you! It's been a while since we last spoke, so I just wanted to check in and see if you've come across any interesting B2B product leadership roles. Happy to chat if anything relevant has come up on your end.

Note: this register deliberately uses the warm connectors ("I hope the year is off to a great start," "just wanted to check in") that are banned for cold outreach. That's correct here. Don't strip them.

## Reschedule / cancel a meeting

Gracious and brief. If the other side moved it: "No worries at all" + confirm the new time. If the user is moving it: a one-line honest reason, then propose an alternative or drop the Calendly link. No over-apology. Real example: "Thanks, I received your invite! However, there are some unforeseen changes on my side and I'm afraid I'll be on the go then. Are you available [alternative]?"

(Note: here "circling back" is a deliberate self-aware joke. He does use "circle back" plainly too - it's not banned - but leaning on the cliche knowingly is a nice move in a warm message.)

## Intro / reference email

**Goal:** connect two people cleanly and get out of the way.

**Structure:**
1. Open with real thanks to the reference/connector ("Thanks again for agreeing to be a reference for me" / "Grateful you're willing to act as a reference" / "Really appreciate you agreeing to help").
2. Introduce: "Please meet [Name], [title] at [Company]" or "Connecting you here with [Name]."
3. State the professional relationship and context in one or two grounded sentences.
4. One sentence on why each person has a relevant perspective. Don't oversell either side.
5. Hand off logistics to them unless the user asked to coordinate.

**Etiquette:** once the intro lands, the introducer gets moved to bcc on the next reply ("moving you to bcc to spare your inbox"). If drafting that follow-up, do this.

**Example:**
> Hi both,
>
> Thanks again for making time on this. Please meet Sam (cc'd), who led growth at Jooble; Dana (cc'd) is building vertical CRM at [Company].
>
> Sam, Dana's wrestling with the horizontal-vs-vertical roadmap tension you solved last year. Dana, Sam's one of the few people who's actually shipped through it.
>
> I'll let you two take it from here.

## Scheduling

**Goal:** lock a time with minimum back-and-forth.

**Structure:** state availability or drop the Calendly link clearly. The user's default move is to share his standing Calendly link and invite the person to grab a slot ("Here is my Calendly: [link] - feel free to book a suitable time"). Mention a constraint only if it actually helps (timezone, a hard window). No apologies, no over-explanation.

**Example:**
> Hi Dana,
>
> Happy to connect. Here's my Calendly so you can grab whatever works: https://calendly.com/a-orlov/online-meeting. I'm in Kyiv time, so earlier in your day is easiest on my end.

**Real-time micro-logistics (live thread, RU/UA).** When a time is being sorted in the moment with someone he's actively talking to, he drops to a single line, no signature, matching the speed of the exchange: `Давай в 17:00 за тим же посиланням зайду?` / `у тебя будет еще время сегодня позднее? Через 15 минут и позже?` Don't wrap these in a full email shell.

## Decline

**Goal:** say no cleanly, keep the relationship.

**Structure:** one clear no, one honest brief reason (no essay), optionally a door left open or an alternative. Tighten the tone (see high-stakes modifier). Don't over-apologize; a confident, warm no respects both people more than a guilty one.

**Example:**
> Hi Dana,
>
> Thanks for thinking of me on this. I'm going to pass for now, my focus through Q3 is locked on the current role. If that changes I'll come back to you directly. Genuinely appreciate the offer.

## Bad news

**Goal:** deliver it without burying it or softening it into ambiguity.

**Structure:** lead with the news plainly in the first line or two (don't make them dig for it), then the reason or context, then what happens next or what you're doing about it. Plain words, no corporate euphemism ("learnings," "headwinds"). Honesty calibrated, not brutal.

## Good news / congratulations reply

**Goal:** react to good news (an approval, an offer, a milestone, someone else's win) with genuine warmth, then move to whatever's next. This is the loosen direction, not the tighten one.

**Structure:** warm reaction first (real, not manufactured), brief thanks to anyone who helped, then any precise follow-up question or next step. Doubled exclamation and a celebration emoji are in-voice here for warm contacts; keep them out of cold or official mail. Don't gush past one beat.

His real markers: `Thanks, that's great news!! 🎉🎉 Appreciate your help along this process!` then a clean, specific question if one is needed. RU/UA equivalent: `Супер, дякую!!` / `Чудові новини, дякую!`

## Negotiation / pushback

**Goal:** hold a position without damaging the relationship.

**Structure:** acknowledge their position in one line, state yours clearly with the reasoning behind it, propose a concrete path. Tighten the tone. Confidence and specifics carry more than length. Avoid both aggression and apology; you're stating a position, not asking permission to have one.

**Offer negotiation (his observed pattern):** warm greeting, one line framing ("As promised, coming back with proposed changes to the offer, as we discussed"), then a numbered list of specific, itemized asks (each point named and concrete), then a warm close inviting their response ("Looking forward to your feedback!"). Itemizing keeps a multi-point negotiation clear and unemotional. Keep the warmth in the wrapper, the precision in the list.

## Russian / Ukrainian register (worked example)

A warm ask to a former colleague the user knows well. Note: English professional terms stay in English mid-sentence; greeting is first-name + "привет!"; tasteful emoji; an optional warm PS to someone close. Example shown in Path A form (no signature in body); the standing signature block stays in English in either path.

> [Имя], привет!
>
> Я сейчас на финальном этапе собеседований на роль VP of Product в US-based B2B SaaS компании (Series B). Прошёл все раунды, единственный финалист.
>
> Могу ли попросить тебя дать для меня reference как мой бывший manager? С тобой хотел бы пообщаться их co-founder / CTO. Отдельным письмом сделаю intro.
>
> Если ок - скинь, пожалуйста, пару слотов на этой неделе 🙏

Ukrainian is the same register with Ukrainian spelling ("привіт!", "дякую", "зроблю intro"). Match whichever language the recipient uses.

## Edit existing draft

Not a generation task, a preservation task. Follow Step 4 in SKILL.md: re-read fully, minimum change, preserve voice, don't add, flag don't silently fix, list what changed in 2-3 lines after. The user's words win.
