# Register calibration

How to set warmth and formality from the actual relationship, instead of defaulting to a generic "professional" tone that fits no one.

## The two axes

**Warmth / distance** — how well do they know each other, and how friendly is the existing rapport?
- **Cold** — never interacted. No shared context except what's public.
- **Warm-but-distant** — one prior touch: a mutual connection, met once at an event, they replied once, you were introduced.
- **Established** — ongoing professional relationship, several prior exchanges, mutual recognition.
- **Close** — easy rapport, informal history, you'd grab a coffee.

**Power / seniority** — recipient's position relative to the user.
- **Senior to user / decision-maker** (a CEO the user is courting, an investor, a hiring principal) — more deference, no presumption on their time, the ask is humble and easy to decline.
- **Peer** — symmetric, conversational, direct.
- **Junior to user / a report** — warm, clear, low ceremony; the user can simply state what's needed.

The message inherits tone from the *combination*. Cold + senior is the most careful (specific, brief, low-friction ask, no familiarity). Close + peer is the loosest (skip the greeting, get to the point, contractions, dry humor OK).

## Tone dials by cell

Grounded in the user's actual sent mail. His real greeting is "Hi [First]," in English and "[Name], привет!" / "[Name], привіт!" in Russian/Ukrainian (first name, comma, often an exclamation in RU/UA). He reserves "Dear ..." for official/institutional correspondence only.

**Sign-off is handled outside this table** — see `SKILL.md` Step 3 for the Path A / Path B signature rule (Path A = body ends with last substantive line, Gmail auto-appends signature on send; Path B = inline standing block at the bottom for Gmail API drafts). Both paths use the same signature content (`Best regards, / Alex Orlov, / alexorlov.co | Linkedin`); the table below covers the dials that vary by relationship.

| Relationship | Greeting | Contractions | Ask directness | Emoji | Length |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cold + senior | "Hi [First]," | Yes, light | Soft, optional, easy no | No | Shortest, <120w |
| Cold + peer | "Hi [First]," | Yes | Direct but not presumptuous | No | Short |
| Warm-but-distant | "Hi [First]," / "[Имя], привет!" | Yes | Direct, reference the prior touch | Sparingly if warm | Short |
| Established peer | "Hi [First]," / "[Имя], привет!" | Yes | Direct | OK in RU/UA, light in EN | Medium-short |
| Close peer | "[Имя], привет!" / "Hey [First]," | Yes, freely | Just say it | Yes, natural (🙏😊🤞) | Shortest |
| Report / junior | "Hi [First]," / "[Имя], привет!" | Yes | State it plainly, warm | OK | As needed |
| Official / institutional (embassy, consulate, vendor) | "Dear [Unit/Mr. X]," | Sparing | Precise, factual, polite | Never | As needed, exact |

The official/institutional register is its own thing: "Dear Immigrant Visa Unit," / "Dear Consular Section," then clear, factual, slightly formal prose ("Thanks for your email. Sure, I will fill out the document and send it over."). Polite and precise, no warmth performance, no emoji, no jokes.

Genuine warmth is on-brand, not a violation. In warm contexts the user writes real exclamations ("Большое спасибо!!", "Looking forward to connecting!", "Pleasure to e-meet you!") and sometimes a warm PS to people he's close to. Keep that. What stays banned is *manufactured* enthusiasm toward strangers (see `banned-phrases.md`).

On non-email channels (LinkedIn, WhatsApp, Slack, SMS) there's no auto-signature. Drop one notch of formality, drop the subject line, and use the per-channel signature norms (LinkedIn: `Alex.` / `- Alex.` / no signature on quick replies — see `linkedin.md`; chat: usually no signature).

Three nuances from the wider sample:
- **Bare first name in ongoing threads.** Once a thread is going, he frequently drops "Hi" and opens with just "[First]," ("Andrew,", "Maggie,", "Manuel,", "Kayla, thanks for pointing this out"). Use the bare-name opener for continuing replies; reserve "Hi [First]," for the first message.
- **Ukrainian register.** "вітаю!" is his semi-formal/professional Ukrainian greeting (HR, mentees, vendors, offer talks); "привіт!" is the casual one. "Дякую, і до зустрічі!" is a natural warm close.
- **A single wry emoji can appear even in English professional mail** to a warmish contact (he opened a recruiter note with a self-deprecating "😐"). Rare and dry, never a string, never to a cold senior stranger or in official correspondence.

## Reading register off a prior thread

When replying, the recipient has already set the register. Match it; don't reset it to your default.

- **Their greeting and sign-off** — if they wrote "Hey" and signed just their first name, mirror that. If they wrote "Dear [Full Name]" and "Kind regards," stay formal. Mirror, don't exceed.
- **Their sentence length and contractions** — terse and clipped vs. expansive. Match the rhythm.
- **What they actually asked** — answer the specific open question first. A reply that ignores their last question reads as not having read it.
- **Their warmth trend** — if each exchange has gotten warmer, you can match the new level. If they stayed crisp, stay crisp.
- **Their language** — if they switched to Russian/Ukrainian/English, follow.

Mirroring slightly *under* their warmth is safer than overshooting. Coming in warmer than the relationship supports is the thing that reads as fake.

## High-stakes modifier

Declining, delivering bad news, pushing back, or negotiating: tighten regardless of relationship. Shorter sentences, plainer words, fewer adjectives, no over-apology, no nervous hedging ("I'm so sorry to bother you, I totally understand if..."). Confidence here reads as respect. One clear position, one clean reason, done. Detail in `message-playbooks.md`.

The inverse also holds: good news, congratulations, and rapport messages to people he knows loosen, not tighten. A doubled exclamation and a single celebration emoji (`🎉`) are in-voice for warm contacts here. Keep the loosening to people he actually knows; cold or official recipients stay measured even when the news is good.

## Follow-up cadence

A follow-up is not a re-send. Rules:
- Don't repeat the previous message or quote it back at them.
- Don't lead with apology ("Sorry to chase," "I know you're busy"). One light acknowledgment at most, or none.
- Add something new: a fresh angle, a relevant development, a narrower ask, or a genuine reason the timing changed.
- Be forward-looking and short. Make the next step obvious. Confident, not pushy.
- Spacing: a few days for the first nudge, then widen. After two unanswered follow-ups, stop or change channel rather than sending a third.
