# Enterprise workflow AI equilibrium — research brief

_question: How will enterprise workflows change due to AI — humans avoiding fully agentic (no SaaS UIs needed), AI as NL layer to interact with SaaS, single AI chat by platform providers vs SaaS-embedded chats, deterministic workflows vs agents, SaaS vs cheap agent-driven custom builds — where do people see the equilibrium? Consider different CRM / operational / other workflow types._
_date: 2026-06-18 · sources: 12 · provenance: [drop](../../../../_inbox/processed/tg-20260617-202524-112.md)_

## TL;DR

The "NL layer replaces SaaS UI" thesis is directionally supported but early: Gartner forecasts <5% enterprise agentic deployment in 2025 rising to 40% by 2026, and IDC found 80%+ of enterprises already reconsidering packaged app investments. The equilibrium is not SaaS dying — it is SaaS becoming backend infrastructure while AI orchestrates on top. CRM/sales is the furthest along (Salesforce Agentforce: 18,500 customers, 3B monthly workflows); ERP/finance has the weakest evidence due to compliance requirements and data-lock dynamics. Platform incumbents (SAP, Salesforce, Microsoft) are actively walling off third-party AI agents to capture the orchestration layer, which is the sharpest near-term threat to independent SaaS vendors.

## What's known

1. **Gartner: <5% → 40% agentic enterprise deployment 2025–2026** [Fact / Gartner via IntelligentHQ, 2025]: Less than 5% of enterprise applications had a task-oriented AI agent in 2025; Gartner projects 40% by 2026. Databricks reported a 327% increase in multi-agent system usage in the four months ending January 2026. [Inference] Adoption is accelerating faster than most product roadmaps assume.

2. **IDC "agent takeover" thesis: AI becomes the primary user of enterprise software** [Fact / IDC, Feb 2025 survey of ~900 companies]: 80%+ of companies believe "AI agents are the new enterprise apps, triggering reconsidering of our investments in packaged apps." IDC predicts that by 2028, 70% of software vendors will abandon pure seat-based pricing for consumption/outcome metrics. [Practitioner consensus / IDC]: Agentic "flow-of-work" UX will replace much of today's direct SaaS interaction — the interface abstraction thesis is IDC's framing, not just VC speculation.

3. **CRM/sales: strongest evidence, already in production** [Fact / Salesforce Q1 2026 earnings + SaaStr case study]: Salesforce Agentforce reached 18,500 customers with 3B monthly workflows — production scale, not preview. SaaStr deployed Agentforce for win-back campaigns (72% open rates). HubSpot AI sales features: 48% decrease in average time to close, 76% of sales pros report more time selling. Futurum Q1 2026 survey: 74% of enterprises plan to switch or consider switching vendors by 2028; 39% expect GenAI delivery via task-automating agents. [Inference] CRM is the workflow type where NL-layer thesis has moved from thesis to measurable ROI.

4. **ERP/finance/HR: weakest evidence; compliance and data gravity slow substitution** [Practitioner consensus / Bessemer State of AI 2025; Kai Waehner blog, May 2026]: Bessemer names next-gen ERP tools (Everest, Doss, Rillet) as "systems of action" automating financial forecasting and procurement — but these are greenfield, not replacements of SAP/Oracle. ⚠ Gap: no published case studies of agentic AI measurably replacing ERP workflows at production scale in regulated industries. [Inference] High compliance, auditability requirements, and entrenched data gravity make ERP the last domain to fully agenticize.

5. **Sequoia thesis: "Services: The New Software" (Julien Bek, April 2026)** [Primary / Sequoia Capital]: The copilot/autopilot framework — copilots sell tools, autopilots sell finished work (outcomes). Core claim: for every $1 spent on software, $6 is spent on services; AI can now capture the services budget. Highest-conviction verticals: recruiting/staffing ($200B+), legal transactional work, IT managed services ($100B+) — all high-volume, language-heavy, output-verifiable workflows. [Inference] This maps to B2B SaaS categories with the thinnest UI differentiation and most commoditized workflows.

6. **a16z thesis: "AI Will Eat Application Software" (early 2026)** [Primary / a16z]: Core concept: "process power" — application software encodes organizational process opinions that compound over time. The defensible AI layer is not the model but the orchestration layer that routes model output through domain-specific professional workflows. Named example: Eve (insurance claims — voice agent contacts customers, sorts medical records, drafts claim letters; system of record through end-to-end workflow ownership). [Inference] a16z's framework suggests moats shift from UI/UX to workflow depth and data ownership — not NL layer itself but who controls the process graph.

7. **Bessemer: systems of record → systems of action** [Primary / Bessemer State of AI 2025]: Named "system of action" examples in CRM: Day.ai, Attio (auto-log customer interactions). ERP: Everest, Doss, Rillet (automate financial forecasting, procurement). Bessemer's thesis is vertical AI eclipsing legacy vertical SaaS in language-heavy, manual workflows. Two archetypes: "Supernovas" ($100M ARR in 1.5 years, fragile retention) vs "Shooting Stars" ($3M→$100M over 4 years, strong PMF). ⚠ Gap: Bessemer does not quantify which workflow types have stronger defensibility.

8. **Deterministic wins for known, repeatable, low-stakes processes; agents win for ambiguous reasoning tasks** [Practitioner consensus / svahnar.com analysis; Composio, 2025]: Deterministic platforms (n8n, Zapier, Make) are appropriate when every step is known, stakes are low, and reliability is required. All three platforms have added AI nodes (n8n 2.0: 70+ AI/LangChain nodes; Zapier Agents: 8,000+ apps; Make: Maia NL scenario builder) but retain their deterministic core. Failure mode: applying agentic tools to deterministic problems adds cost, latency, complexity without value. Named migration threshold: teams outgrow Zapier/Make/n8n when the workflow requires reasoning about what to do next — Composio documents this as the "production migration" pattern.

9. **Platform providers are building walls: SAP API lockdown as the clearest example** [Fact / SAP API policy published April 29, 2026; enforcement June 9, 2026; Forrester analysis]: SAP's updated API policy explicitly prohibits "interaction or integration with (semi-)autonomous or generative AI systems" using SAP APIs unless routed through Joule/Business Data Cloud/Agent Gateway. DSAG Investment Survey 2026: only 3% of SAP customers use Joule in production; 77% of AI-active SAP customers use Microsoft Copilot — which SAP just restricted. Forrester: "SAP is attempting to become the gatekeeper of enterprise AI — CIOs should push back." Salesforce and ServiceNow have not followed suit (Techzine, 2026). [Inference] Platform API gatekeeping is the mechanism by which incumbents will attempt to own the orchestration layer. This is the most concrete threat to independent SaaS vendors building on top of platform data.

10. **Market reaction: $285B SaaS market rout on Anthropic enterprise agent launch (Feb 2026)** [Fact / Fortune, Feb 2026]: ServiceNow -7%, Salesforce -7%, Intuit -11% on Anthropic's enterprise plugins release. Earlier, SAP cloud revenue forecast disappointment and ServiceNow earnings miss triggered a $300B SaaS market cap drop (Jan 2026). Despite the selloffs, Salesforce/ServiceNow/Microsoft are actively building agent layers — the threat is not replacement but margin compression and interface disintermediation. [Inference] Markets are pricing in seat-based pricing erosion faster than enterprises are actually migrating.

11. **Platform AI chat (Einstein, Copilot, Joule) vs SaaS-embedded agents: a zero-sum fight for the interaction layer** [Inference from multiple sources]: Platform incumbents are building single-pane-of-glass AI chat to capture all workflow intent signals (Salesforce Einstein/Agentforce, Microsoft 365 Copilot, SAP Joule). Independent SaaS vendors face a binary: embed deeply enough into workflow data to justify a separate agent, or become a backend API that the platform's AI orchestrates. [Practitioner consensus / a16z, Bessemer]: The winners will be vendors who own the system of record for a workflow — the interaction layer follows the data, not the UX.

12. **Custom agent builds vs SaaS: cost arbitrage real but not yet production-proven at scale** [Inference / multiple sources; ⚠ gap]: The "cheap agent-driven custom build replacing SaaS" thesis is plausible for simple, narrow, non-compliance-sensitive workflows (internal tools, data pipelines, notification systems). No published evidence of enterprises replacing core SaaS (CRM, ERP, HRIS) with custom agent builds at scale. The risk: maintenance cost, reliability, and compliance overhead compound quickly.

## Gaps & caveats

- ⚠ ERP/finance/HR agentic case studies are almost entirely absent from published literature — the strongest claims come from greenfield startups, not legacy ERP replacement.
- ⚠ "NL layer replacing UI" evidence is mostly survey data and VC thesis, not measured user behavior studies. Controlled usability research comparing NL agents to SaaS UIs does not yet exist at scale.
- ⚠ Custom agent build vs SaaS cost comparison: no published TCO studies with real enterprise data; the "AI makes custom builds cheap" claim is largely based on coding productivity gains, not operational/compliance cost modeling.
- ⚠ Benn Stancil's "SaaS 2.0" framework was inaccessible during research (Substack paywall) — likely contains a nuanced counterargument to the pure disruption narrative.
- Most VC theses (a16z, Sequoia, Bessemer) are written by investors with portfolio stakes in AI-native software — directionally credible but not neutral.
