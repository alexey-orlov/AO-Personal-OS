## The throughline
The media landscape has inverted: where legacy broadcast media demanded corporate polish and atomic soundbites, new media rewards sustained human conversations and strong personal identities. The unified playbook has four beats. (1) Lead with the person — audiences follow individuals (Elon, Palmer, Alex Karp) not logos, so deciding who carries the company's narrative publicly is a foundational strategic choice. (2) Go direct — own your channels (podcasts, newsletters, Substack, posts) rather than depending on legacy press to carry your message; this gives control over context and cadence. (3) Message before distribution — crystallize the desired outcome (hiring, fundraising, enterprise sales) and the audience it must move before chasing reach; distribution multiplies the message, good or bad. (4) Be interesting enough to be hated — neutral audiences mean invisibility; strategic controversy amplifies reach and builds distinct brand identity.

## Insights

### Authentic, conversational public speaking outperforms staged corporate rhetoric
Media coaching that teaches people to speak as if they were at lunch with a friend produces better outcomes than classic polished 'anchorman' delivery. The trainer Les Elden advised CEOs to only speak publicly about things they know intimately and to communicate them viscerally and personally; that approach makes speakers feel like real people and builds trust on long‑form platforms like podcasts. This matters because new media favors sustained, human conversations over sanitized one‑minute soundbites.

### In new media the company brand becomes the founder-person brand
With centralized broadcast media, corporate brands were distilled into atomic messages to squeeze through narrow channels; now the opposite is true — the visible individual (Elon, Palmer, Alex Karp) is the brand and carries the company's narrative. The panel argues that winning marketing today means making a person interesting and recognizable because audiences follow people across long-form formats, not abstractions. That shift forces startups to decide who will be the enduring, public-facing personality rather than hiding behind a corporate logo.

### 'Go direct': tell your own story through your channels and allies
Legacy press can no longer be relied on to run a company's narrative or strategy, so founders must 'go direct' — using company channels, allies, podcasts, newsletters, and Substack to control context and cadence. The panel highlights the rise of long-form podcasts and the success of firms that invest in their own publishing and narrative work (they cite a SpaceX post they published as an example). Going direct lets you run a sustained playbook instead of hoping for a favorable legacy-media hit.

### Be interesting — neutral audiences mean you're invisible
New media rewards strong, polarizing positions: the panel argues it's better to be loved and hated than ignored because controversy amplifies attention and builds brand identity. They give examples — a public kerfuffle with The New York Times produced the firm's biggest post at the time — and caution founders to pick fights strategically so they amplify your message, not somebody else's. The practical takeaway is: embrace distinctiveness and expect backlash as a sign you're making an impact.

### Message first, distribution second — founders overvalue tactics
A common mistake is chasing distribution (going viral, booking big podcasts) before crystallizing the strategic message you want to amplify; distribution merely multiplies the message's effect. Gabby warns that many founders try to say everything at once instead of defining the outcome they need (hiring, enterprise sales, fundraising) and tailoring a narrative to move that audience. The correct sequence is: pick the desired outcome, craft a focused, interesting message that fits that audience, then deploy channels to scale it.
