### Top items to study this session

#### 1. 🟡 Non-native verb-preposition pairings — 3 instances

English verbs have rigid preposition pairings; word-by-word translation from RU/UA often produces collocations that sound off to native ears. Pick the right preposition (or no preposition) per verb, not by analogy.

- "**reiterate on** what was the conversation" → "**recap** the conversation" / "**walk me through** the conversation"
- "if you can **elaborate that**" → "if you can **elaborate on** that"
- "I need to **scale on** that approach" → "I need to **build on** / **double down on** that approach"

**Drill:** *elaborate on*, *build on*, *reflect on*, *touch on*, *expand on* — these take *on*. *Reiterate* and *recap* are transitive — no preposition. Before saying a verb + preposition, mentally check: "have I actually heard a native pair this verb with this preposition?"

#### 2. 🟡 Conditional with "will/'ll" after "if" — 2 instances

In a real conditional, the *if*-clause uses the **present tense**, never *will* or *'ll*. The future-meaning goes into the main clause only. This is a classic Slavic-speaker error and very audible.

- "if **you'll be working** on something" → "if **you're working** on something"
- "if **you'll see** something showing up on your radar" → "if **you see** something showing up on your radar"

**Drill:** *If it rains, I'll stay home.* (not *if it will rain*). When tempted to put *'ll* after *if*, ask: "is this a normal conditional?" — if yes, strip the *'ll*.

#### 3. 🟡 Polite "would" with *appreciate* / *like* — 1 instance (high-leverage)

To sound polite in English, *appreciate* and *like* take **would/'d**, not *will/'ll*. "I'll appreciate" sounds like a strange promise about your future emotions; "I'd appreciate" is the standard polite request.

- "**I'll appreciate** maybe if you can reiterate" → "**I'd appreciate it** if you could recap"

**Drill:** *I'd appreciate it if…*, *I'd like to…*, *I'd love to…*, *I'd prefer…*. These are fixed politeness frames — always *'d*, never *'ll*.

#### 4. 🟡 Indirect question — no subject-verb inversion — 1 instance (high-leverage)

In a direct question you invert ("What **was** the conversation?"). Once the question is embedded inside another clause, you **undo the inversion** ("…recap what the conversation **was**"). Keeping the inversion is one of the most recognisable non-native tells.

- "reiterate on **what was the conversation**" → "recap **what the conversation was**"

**Drill:** Mental test: would this work as a standalone question? If yes (*what was the conversation?*) and you're embedding it, flip back to statement order (*what the conversation was*). Same rule for *who*, *where*, *why*, *how*.

#### 5. 🟡 Non-native word choice / false friend — 3 instances

A handful of words mean something different in English than the cognate suggests, or are simply not used the way RU/UA would use them.

- "Besides this **outstanding one**" → "Besides this **one glaring exception**" (*outstanding* in English means *excellent*, not *remaining/exceptional*)
- "some nuance in the **personas** they see fit" → "some nuance in the **type of person** they're looking for" (*personas* in English = marketing/UX archetypes, not candidates)
- "Maybe **on the contrary**, something that…" → "**Or on the flip side**, something that…" (*on the contrary* contradicts a prior statement; here you meant *conversely*)

**Drill:** *outstanding* = excellent (not remaining). *Persona* = product-marketing archetype. *On the contrary* directly contradicts what was just said — for "or the opposite", use *conversely* / *on the flip side* / *the other way around*.

#### 6. 🟡 Set-phrase article/plural error — 1 instance (high-leverage)

Idiomatic English phrases have frozen forms — the article and plural are fixed. "Behind the scenes" is always plural in this idiom.

- "the conversation **behind the scene**" → "the conversation **behind the scenes**"

**Drill:** *behind the scenes*, *the powers that be*, *the ins and outs*, *at the end of the day*. Treat idioms as one word — memorise the exact form.

#### 7. 🟡 Non-native conversational opener — 1 instance

"How is your routine?" is not a native English small-talk greeting — it sounds like a literal translation. Use a standard opener.

- "And **how is your routine?**" → "And **how have you been?**" / "**how are things on your end?**" / "**how's everything?**"

**Drill:** Standard openers: *How have you been? · How are things? · How's everything going? · What's new?* Don't translate Russian/Ukrainian small-talk phrases directly — pick from this fixed set.
