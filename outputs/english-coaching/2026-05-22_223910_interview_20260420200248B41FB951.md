## Executive summary

Most of the load is in **Section 1 (Wrong words / collocations)** and **Section 4 (Grammar)**, driven by two engines: RU/UA-flavoured calques (*in the nearest future*, *absolutely different*, *from one side / on the other side*, *economical terms*) and chronic article omission on countable singulars (*major presence*, *entrepreneurial type of thing*, *low-effort approach*, *good number*). A handful of full sentence-collapses (*"there's 3 divisions are still alive"*, *"I kind of evolved from my initial ideas and assumptions what might be the ideas for the strategy look like"*) are the most embarrassing — they happen in long, run-on thoughts. **Bottom-line fix:** shorten sentences in dense explanations and audit them for missing articles. ## Top items to study this session

#### 1. 🟡 Missing article on countable singular nouns — 15+ instances

You drop *a / the* in front of countable singulars, especially in long explanatory sentences. This is the single biggest "non-native tell" in the transcript. Default to inserting an article before any countable noun unless it's plural or generic-uncountable.

- "They have **major presence** in Western Europe" → They have **a major presence**
- "it was really **entrepreneurial type of thing**" → it was **an entrepreneurial type of thing**
- "we started with **low-effort approach**" → we started with **a low-effort approach**
- "I'm not sure it's actually **working model**" → I'm not sure it's actually **a working model**

**Drill:** before pressing send on a sentence, scan every noun. If it's a thing you could count ("one approach", "one model", "one presence"), put *a/an/the* in front. Especially after *with*, *as*, *into*, *from*, *like*.

#### 2. 🔴 Sentence-collapse on long explanations — 3 instances

When sentences run past ~20 words you sometimes lose the syntactic thread and the verb structure breaks. Native listeners notice this strongly. Plant a hard period earlier.

- "I kind of evolved from my initial ideas and assumptions **what might be the ideas for the strategy look like**" → …**about what the strategy might look like**
- "**there's 3 divisions are still alive** within company's portfolio" → **there are 3 divisions still alive** within the company's portfolio
- "Because **initial insight was some kind of the community component into that**" → Because **the initial insight was about adding some kind of community component**

**Drill:** if a sentence has more than one *that* / *what* / *which*, stop and break it into two. The clauses lose track of each other otherwise.

#### 3. 🟡 False friends (RU/UA influence) — 4 patterns

Russian/Ukrainian roots that look identical to English words but carry a different sense. These are easy wins once internalised.

- "in **the nearest future**" → in **the near future** (ближайшее → *near*, not *nearest*)
- "**absolutely different** people" → **completely different** people (абсолютно ≠ *absolutely* here)
- "works in **economical terms**" → works in **economic terms** (*economical* = thrifty; *economic* = financial)
- "**from one side**… **on the other side**" → **on one hand**… **on the other hand**

**Drill:** memorise the four pairs above. Add to mental blocklist alongside the *actual → current* pair you already know.

#### 4. 🟡 Indirect questions — no subject-verb inversion — 4 instances

Inside *I don't know / we need to understand / let's see*, English does NOT invert. It uses statement word order.

- "we need to understand **what are the channels** that drive that supply" → **what the channels are**
- "I don't know **how huge is that**" → **how big it is** (and prefer *big*/*large* — *how huge* is unidiomatic)
- "see whether you're gonna have higher conversions than **the ones that they have**" → fine, but earlier: "**how that matches with what's the real condition**" → **what the real condition is**

**Drill:** "I don't know **where she is**" — not "where is she". When the question is *inside* another sentence, write the subject first, then the verb.

#### 5. 🟡 Adverb placement around auxiliaries — 5 instances

Adverbs (*fully*, *actually*, *also*, *definitely*) go between the subject and the main verb when there's an auxiliary, not before *is/are/will*.

- "**it fully is** based on the assumption" → **it is fully based** on
- "**are they interested actually** in having us as a channel" → are they **actually interested** in
- "**it should be also added** to the equation" → it **should also be added** to
- "**there will be definitely the deviation**" → there **will definitely be some deviation**
- "what we can do is **before launching actually this model**" → before **actually launching** this model

**Drill:** the slot for these adverbs is *aux + ADV + verb*. "She **has always** liked it." "It **will definitely** work."

#### 6. 🟡 Wrong prepositions in collocations — 6 instances

- "seeking **for** part-time gigs" → seeking part-time gigs (seek is transitive — no *for*)
- "subject **for** only the first risk" → subject **to**
- "**at** the surface" → **on** the surface
- "compensate the switching cost" → compensate **for** the switching cost
- "trust **in**" the data → trust the data (transitive)
- "the other approach… **except** the partnership" → **besides / other than** the partnership

**Drill:** *seek X*, *trust X*, *compensate for X*, *subject to X*, *on the surface*, *besides X*. Drill the verb together with its preposition, not separately.

#### 7. 🔴 Mangled set phrase: "Having said that" — 1 instance

- "**Having that said**, 20% year-over-year growth is again a good number" → **Having said that**

**Drill:** the fixed order is *Having said that* (= "despite what I just said"). Same as *That said, …*.

#### 8. 🔴 Wrong word in fixed slot — 2 distinct misuses

Two micro-errors that jarred badly because they sit in slots a native speaker would never put them in.

- "I kind of have **a feature** that I structure everything **in written**" → I have **a habit** of structuring everything **in writing**
- "in this model, we cannot **upfront** the true condition of the item" → we cannot **know upfront** the true condition (*upfront* is an adverb/adjective, not a verb)

**Drill:** *feature* describes a product/system, not a personal trait — use *habit*, *tendency*, *practice*. *In writing* (preposition + gerund), never *in written*. *Upfront* is never a verb — say *know upfront* / *determine upfront*.
