## Executive summary

The most loaded category by far is **§4 Grammar**, driven almost entirely by missing articles before singular countable nouns (~14 instances) and a long tail of preposition-in-collocation slips. §1 has a smaller but notable cluster of false friends and mangled set phrases (*as following*, *classical* for *classic*, *going an extra mile*). The single most important fix is the article problem — it's the most frequent non-native marker in this call by a wide margin, and most instances are mechanical (just insert *a/an/the* before the singular noun). Two short hard-grammar rules to nail next: no future *will* after *if*, and *help + bare infinitive* (not *-ing*). Severity totals: 🔴 5 · 🟡 27 · 🟢 12.

## Top items to study this session

#### 1. 🟡 Missing article on a singular countable noun — 14 instances

By a wide margin the dominant marker of non-native speech in this call. English requires *a/an/the* before almost every singular countable noun. Slavic-language speakers systematically drop them; native speakers register the absence immediately.

- "Quite **busy** day" → Quite **a busy** day
- "kind of **interesting meeting**" → kind of **an interesting meeting**
- "appreciate you taking **time**" → appreciate you taking **the time**
- "with **fintech** startup" → with **a fintech** startup
- "to **no-code** workflow automation platform" → to **a no-code** workflow automation platform
- "in **Netherlands**" → in **the Netherlands** (countries that are plural/collective always take *the*)
- "you have **lightning-fast** feedback loop" → you have **a lightning-fast** feedback loop
- "plus **Agentic** layer" → plus **an Agentic** layer
- "I was under **assumption**" → I was under **the assumption**

**Drill:** Before every singular countable noun, ask: *which one — a generic one (a/an), or a specific one (the)?* If you can't easily say "this one, the one we just talked about", default to *a/an*. The set phrases *under the assumption*, *the extra mile*, *the same page*, *the bottom line* always take *the*.

#### 2. 🔴 No future *will* after *if* in conditionals — 1 instance

Hard grammar rule: in conditional clauses, use the present tense after *if*, even when talking about the future. This is one of the fastest "tells" of non-native English.

- "**if you'll have** 1 minute more" → **if you have** one more minute

**Drill:** *If* + present, *then* + will. "If it **rains**, we **will** stay home." Never "If it will rain."

#### 3. 🔴 *Help* + bare infinitive (no *-ing*) — 1 instance

After *help (someone) do something*, the second verb is the bare infinitive. *Help …ing* is wrong.

- "where it might **help us accelerating**" → where it might **help us accelerate**

**Drill:** *help us ship*, *help us decide*, *help us grow* — never *help us shipping/deciding/growing*.

#### 4. 🔴 Count vs. uncount: *little/much* with mass nouns, *few/many* with countables — 2 instances

*Room*, *time*, *information*, *advice*, *work* are uncountable → *little/much*. *Conversations*, *meetings*, *roadblocks* are countable → *few/many*. Mixing them is a clear grammar error.

- "we left so **few room**" → we left so **little room** (room is uncountable here)
- "didn't have **much conversations**" → didn't have **many conversations**

**Drill:** Mass nouns (no plural form) → **little/much/a lot of**. Plural countables → **few/many/a lot of**. *A lot of* is the safe escape hatch if unsure.

#### 5. 🔴 *As follows*, not *as following* — 1 instance

Frozen set phrase. The verb form is *follows* (3rd-person singular present), not the participle.

- "they are **as following**" → they are **as follows**

**Drill:** "My points are **as follows**: …" Memorise as one unit.

#### 6. 🟡 Wrong preposition in fixed verb/noun collocations — 7 instances

Each verb or noun in English has a "married" preposition; using the wrong one sounds non-native even when meaning is clear. RU/UA influence pushes you toward *of/with/in*; English often wants *on/about/at/for*.

- "spent all my career **∅** technology product management" → spent all my career **in** technology product management
- "how do you feel **with** all that difference" → how do you feel **about** all those differences
- "building empathy **to** the customer" → building empathy **with/for** the customer
- "your focus **of** driving GMV" → your focus **on** driving GMV
- "different **in** different times" → different **at** different times
- "elaborate a little bit more **∅** how" → elaborate a little bit more **on** how
- "really being focused **in** those growth metrics" → really being focused **on** those growth metrics

**Drill:** Memorise as units, not as verb + preposition: *focus on*, *empathy with*, *career in*, *frustrated with*, *at the moment*, *at different times*, *elaborate on*. When unsure, *about* is the safest English default for "regarding".

#### 7. 🟡 False friend: *classical* → *classic* — 3 instances

*Classical* in English means *of antiquity* (classical music, classical philosophy). For "traditional / the standard kind of", use **classic**. RU/UA *классический* maps to both, so the slip is systematic.

- "in the **classical meaning** of that" → in the **classic sense** of that
- "for **classical**, I don't know, ERP, CRM" → for **classic** ERP, CRM
- "improving the **classical ML** capabilities" → improving the **classic ML** capabilities

**Drill:** *Classical* → think Mozart, Aristotle. Anything else → *classic*. *Classic example*, *classic mistake*, *classic SaaS*, *classic CRM*.

#### 8. 🟡 Mangled English idioms (set phrases the brain half-remembers) — 4 instances

Idioms are frozen — the words and the articles are fixed. Substituting near-synonyms or dropping articles breaks them and sounds off.

- "going **an** extra mile" → going **the** extra mile (idiom takes *the*)
- "the **final last** one" → just **the last** one (or *one final question*; not both)
- "a **repeating** theme" → a **recurring** theme
- "**particular roadblockers**" → **specific roadblocks** (*roadblocker* isn't standard)

**Drill:** When you reach for an idiom, freeze the whole phrase: *go **the** extra mile · cut to **the** chase · on **the** same page · at **the** end of **the** day · a recurring theme · a roadblock (no -er)*.
