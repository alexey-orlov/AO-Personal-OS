## Top items to study this session

#### 1. 🟡 Missing article (a / the) — ~17 instances

English count-nouns almost always need an article. Bare singulars like "growth team", "clear understanding", "one-off trigger" sound non-native immediately. This is the single rule that fixes the most issues in this transcript.

- "selecting **particular direction**" → selecting **a particular direction**
- "We need to have **clear understanding**" → We need to have **a clear understanding**
- "if it's about **6-week experiment**" → if it's about **a 6-week experiment**
- "It's not about LLM doing that, it's about **the BI system**" (this one is correct) — but: "Whether it's **one-off trigger**" → Whether it's **a one-off trigger**
- "in **the Excel**" → in **Excel** (proper noun, no article) · "I will share the presentation in **the Miro**" → in **Miro**

**Drill:** before every singular count-noun, ask: definite (specific, known to listener) → *the*; indefinite (any one) → *a/an*; proper noun → no article. If you'd say "the framework" in your head but only "framework" comes out, slow down and insert the article.

#### 2. 🟡 Wrong preposition: "subject for X" → "subject to X" — 5 instances

In American English, "subject **to**" means "liable to / affected by / open to". "Subject **for**" is non-existent in this sense. This appears in every paragraph about the framework.

- "definitely **subject for consideration**" → **subject to consideration** (or **a subject for consideration**, with article — different meaning)
- "many activities are not **subject for automation**" → **subject to automation**
- "some actions here that are potentially **subject for LLM**" → **subject to LLM** (replacement)
- "this whole idea is **subject to be revised**" → **subject to revision**
- "that's a **subject for validation**" → **a topic for validation** (or **subject to validation**)

**Drill:** "subject **to**" + noun (e.g., *subject to approval, subject to change, subject to review*). The only time you say "subject **for**" is when "subject" is a noun ("a subject for discussion") — and even there, "topic" is more natural.

#### 3. 🟡 Set-phrase article/plural mangled — 7 instances

English idioms are fixed: change one word and they sound off. You're swapping articles or singular/plural inside well-known set phrases.

- "do you want to **set up a stage** for us" → **set the stage**
- "or I can **take a lead** and guide us" → **take the lead**
- "we don't want to **miss on** that part" → **miss out on**
- "**as I know**, are already focusing heavily" → **as far as I know**
- "do all these designs **in vacuum**" → **in a vacuum**
- "if we **go an extra mile**" → **go the extra mile**
- "**In some words**, there might be some insights" → **In other words** (or **In a few words**)

**Drill:** these are memorize-the-exact-form items. Quick-recall list: *set the stage · take the lead · miss out on · as far as I know · in a vacuum · go the extra mile · in other words · behind the scenes · the task at hand*.

#### 4. 🟡 Countable / uncountable confusion — 5 instances

Mass nouns (work, room, progress, common sense, advice) don't take "a/an" and don't pluralise. You're treating them as countable.

- "or maybe in some cases that's **a work** to be done" → that's **work** to be done
- "so maybe there is **a room** for that negotiation" → there is **room**
- "**the common sense** behind any automation" → **common sense**
- "it's already **a great progress**" → **great progress**
- "ground plumbing **works**" → groundwork (or **plumbing work**)

**Drill:** no "a/an" and no plural on: *work, advice, information, progress, research, feedback, equipment, knowledge, room (= space)*. If you mean a specific instance, say "a piece of work / a piece of advice / a piece of feedback".

#### 5. 🟡 Conditional with "will" after "if" — 3 instances

In a first conditional, the "if" clause stays in present; "will" only goes in the main clause.

- "**If we will be able to** deliver high-quality outputs there, then it's gonna be the answer" → **If we are able to** deliver…
- "if **the reality will be that** — or for example…" → if **reality is that** / if **the reality turns out to be**
- "**if I was doing** that on my own, **it's going to be definitely** less than 40 slides" → **if I'd done** that on my own, **it would definitely have been** less than 40 (mixed/wrong tense throughout)

**Drill:** *If + present, will + base* (first conditional). *If + past, would + base* (second). The "if" clause never gets "will" unless it means "be willing to" ("if you will sign here").

#### 6. 🔴 Russian calque: "feel myself a part of" — 1 instance

Direct translation of "чувствовать себя частью". English doesn't take a reflexive after *feel* in this sense, and doesn't use "a part" — it uses "like part of" or "part of".

- "I am kind of almost already **feeling myself a part of the challenges** you guys are facing" → I'm already starting to **feel like part of the challenges** you guys are facing

**Drill:** *feel* takes no reflexive in English. Say *I feel tired / I feel like part of the team / I feel at home* — never *I feel myself X*. The "myself" is the giveaway of a Slavic-language background.

#### 7. 🔴 Broken adverb placement / missing verb — 1 instance

Stacking adverbs ("also", "probably") between subject and modal, and dropping the "to be" before "able", produces a sentence the listener has to re-parse.

- "**you also want probably be able to** make better decisions" → **you probably also want to be able to** make better decisions

**Drill:** the slot for *probably/usually/also* is **after the first auxiliary** (or before the main verb if there's no auxiliary): *I have probably also seen this · I probably also want to be able to…* Lock the adverbs in that slot before opening your mouth.

#### 8. 🔴 Mangled comparative: "as cool as Claude helped me" — 1 instance

"As cool as" is a comparative of degree ("X is as cool as Y"). You meant "in the same way that" / "just as". The current sentence makes no parseable sense to a native listener.

- "So **as cool as Claude helped me with** the presentation preparation for today… it can help us with pulling together the compelling case" → **Just as Claude helped me with** the presentation preparation for today… it can help us with…

**Drill:** to introduce an analogy, use *Just as X did Y, Z can do W* or *In the same way that X did Y, Z can do W*. Reserve *as X as Y* only for direct comparisons of degree (*as fast as, as cool as, as cheap as*).
