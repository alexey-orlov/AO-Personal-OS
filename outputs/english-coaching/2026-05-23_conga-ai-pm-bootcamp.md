#### 1. 🟡 Missing article (a / the) — ~20 instances

The single most frequent issue in the transcript. Every "the agent", "the SQL query", "the AI" was said as "agent", "SQL query", "AI" — across nearly every slide.

- "this skill links **agent** to some other parts" → links **the agent** to…
- "actually you just define meticulously and thoroughly the workflow **how you want your agent**" → how you want **the agent**
- "ask **agent** to draft me **PRD**" → ask **the agent** to draft me **a PRD**
- "is **so-called self-learning mechanism**" → is **the so-called self-learning mechanism**
- "**Agent** should have the skill to analyze activation" → **The agent** should have…
- "**SQL query** will be executed" → **the SQL query** will be executed
- "with **human** in the loop" → with **a human** in the loop / **the human** in the loop
- "we **do not move forward without that context**" — fine, but: "if it's a certain level of **minor bugs** or certain level of **minor improvements**, it can produce PRs" → certain level of **minor bugs** ✓ but earlier "**bug report or minor user story**" → **a bug report or a minor user story**

**Drill:** when you're about to say "agent", "AI", "user", "system", "PRD", "Jira" + a singular noun — pause. *Agent* and *AI* in particular are count nouns here (a specific one). Default: **the** when you've referred to it before, **a** when introducing it.

#### 2. 🟡 Wrong preposition: "take a look on" → "take a look at" — 6 instances

You "take a look **at**" something in English, not "**on**" something. This is the single most repeated tic in the transcript.

- "We will **take a look on an example** in around half an hour" → take a look **at** an example
- "design reviewer… **taking a look on your PRD**" → taking a look **at** your PRD
- "if you **take a look on the conversations**" → take a look **at** the conversations
- "Still, you have human in the loop at the end of the pipeline when you **take a look on what was the solution proposed**" → take a look **at** what solution was proposed
- "let's **take a look on what we've got** with our agent" → take a look **at** what we've got
- "I'll **take a look on this**" → take a look **at** this

**Drill:** *look at, take a look at, have a look at* — always *at* (= directed gaze). *Look on* exists but means "to observe passively" or "to view as" ("I look on him as a friend"), which is not what you mean.

#### 3. 🟡 Indirect-question inversion — 6+ instances

In a subordinate clause (after "see / know / understand / ask / figure out…"), keep normal subject-verb order. Don't flip into question word order.

- "to **see what are the use cases**" → see **what the use cases are**
- "**what do we spend most of our time** in the implementations" → **what we spend most of our time on** in the implementations
- "the agents decide **what do they do next**" → decide **what they do next**
- "**what are the use cases** I'm gonna try first" → **what use cases** I'm gonna try first
- "figure out **what are the use cases** where the team uses AI" → figure out **what the use cases** are
- "depending on **what do I have as an input**" → depending on **what I have as input**
- "you take a look on **what was the solution proposed**" → **what solution was proposed**
- "where is that in this list" (when reported) → where that is

**Drill:** *I know what time it is* (not *what time is it*). The question-word-order flip is **only** in direct questions. In any *I see / I want to know / let's figure out / depending on…* clause, subject comes before verb.

#### 4. 🟡 "how X look like" → "what X look like" / "how X look" — 4 instances

"What does X look like?" uses *what*. "How does X look?" uses *how* without *like*. You're combining them.

- "we'll see **how it looks like**" → **what it looks like** / **how it looks**
- "describe **how your layouts look like, how your buttons look like, how your sections look like**" → **what your layouts look like…** / **how your layouts look…**
- "**how the workflow might look like**" → **what the workflow might look like**
- "an example of **how the prompt named Skill for Activation Analysis might look like**" → **what the prompt… might look like**

**Drill:** *like* introduces a comparison/example, so it pairs with *what* (the thing being compared to). Use *how* alone (without *like*) when you're asking about manner. Pick one: *What does it look like?* or *How does it look?* — never both.

#### 5. 🟡 False friend: "actual" → "current" — 3 instances

In American English, *actual* means "real / true", **not** "now-existing". When you mean "now-existing", use **current**. Classic RU/UA false friend (актуальный looks like *actual*, means *current*).

- "questions about our ability to build something with our **actual stack**" → our **current** stack
- "it will have nothing to do with **your actual layout** of your product" → **your current** layout
- "with **your actual capabilities**" → with **your current** capabilities

**Drill:** *current* = now-existing (current stack, current strategy, current quarter). *Actual* = real, as opposed to imaginary (the actual cost vs. the estimate). When tempted to say "actual" → ask: do I mean "real/true" or "now-existing"? If the second → say **current**.

#### 6. 🟡 Conditional with "will" after "if" — 3 instances

First conditional: present tense in the "if" clause, *will* only in the main clause.

- "**If everything will work smoothly**, we will have a quick one iteration" → **If everything works smoothly**, we'll have…
- "if **we will be interacting** with the agent" → if **we're interacting** with the agent
- "we'll try **if we'll have enough time**" → **if we have enough time**
- "Happy to answer your questions **if you'll have more** in the chat" → **if you have more** in the chat

**Drill:** *If + present, will + base form*. Never *if + will*. (Exception: *if you will* = "if you would be so kind" — formal politeness, not future.)

#### 7. 🔴 Wrong word: "hard-scripted" → "hard-coded"

You're talking to a tech audience using AI. "Hard-scripted" is not a term in software; "hard-coded" is. This is a meaning-obscuring slip in front of engineers.

- "It might be **hard-scripted**, the key ones might be defined upfront" → It might be **hard-coded**, the key ones might be defined upfront

**Drill:** *hard-coded* = baked into the source code, not configurable at runtime. *Scripted* exists ("a scripted workflow") but doesn't pair with *hard*. Single-fact memorize.

#### 8. 🔴 Double-negative confusion: "don't hesitate to not postpone"

The two negatives ("don't hesitate" + "not postpone") cancel out and leave the listener parsing for a beat. You meant the opposite of what you said.

- "please **don't hesitate to not postpone them** up until the end of the conversation" → please **don't hesitate to ask them** — don't wait until the end / please **ask them as they come up, don't postpone**

**Drill:** "Don't hesitate to X" already means "feel free to X". So X has to be the action you want done (*ask, interrupt, jump in*), not a negation. If you catch a "not" after "don't hesitate to" — stop and rephrase.
