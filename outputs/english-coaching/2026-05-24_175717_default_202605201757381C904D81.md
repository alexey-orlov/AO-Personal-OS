#### 2. 🟡 Set-phrase mangled — fixed expressions said with the wrong piece — 5 instances

Idiomatic English has dozens of "set phrases" where the article, preposition, or plural is fixed. Substituting close-but-wrong tokens is a classic non-native tell — the listener hears a familiar phrase with one wrong note.

- "**Having that said**, let me introduce myself" → "**Having said that**, let me introduce myself"
- "without that context, piece of context **behind the scene** being put together" → "**behind the scenes**"
- "to do that **not in the vacuum** but with real-world data" → "**not in a vacuum**"
- "based on the task **in hand** right now" → "the task **at hand** right now"
- "don't be **under impression** that it's overly complicated" → "**under the impression**"

**Drill:** Memorise the canonical form, never paraphrase: *Having said that · behind the scenes · in a vacuum · the task at hand · under the impression*. Recite them as fixed strings.

#### 3. 🟡 Wrong preposition in collocation — 4 instances

English verb–preposition pairings often diverge from RU/UA. *Thinking* takes *in* (a direction), not *into*. *Targeted* takes *at*, not *to*. *Career* takes *in* (a field), not *with*.

- "start thinking **into** that direction" → "start thinking **in** that direction"
- "targeted specifically **to** the product managers audience" → "targeted specifically **at** the product manager audience"
- "spent my career **with** B2B SaaS product management" → "spent my career **in** B2B SaaS product management"
- "we are **moving down to** the first section" → "we're **moving on to** the first section"

**Drill:** Memorise as pairs, not isolated verbs: *think in a direction · target at an audience · career in a field · move on to a topic*. The preposition is part of the verb.

#### 4. 🟡 Countable/uncountable confusion — "much of" + countable noun — 3 instances

In English, *much* takes uncountable nouns (*much time, much information*); *many* takes countable nouns (*many answers, many responses*). "Much of responses" is doubly wrong: wrong quantifier AND a stray *of*.

- "I didn't get that **much of responses**" → "I didn't get **many responses**"
- "didn't get **much of answers** in the form" → "didn't get **many answers** in the form"
- "do you see **much of collaboration**" → "do you see **much collaboration**" (collaboration is uncountable — drop the *of*)

**Drill:** Ask: "Can I say *one X*?" If yes → countable → use *many*, never *much*. *One answer, one response, one question* → *many*. And never insert *of* after *much/many* unless followed by *the/my/those* (*many of those answers* ✓).

#### 5. 🟡 False friend (RU/UA): *actual* → *current* — 2 instances

In American English, *actual* means "real/true", not "now-existing". When you mean "as of today / what we have right now", use **current**. Classic RU/UA false friend (актуальный looks like *actual*, means *current*).

- "with our **actual** stack" → "with our **current** stack"
- "your **actual** capabilities" → "your **current** capabilities"

**Drill:** *current product · current stack · current strategy · current quarter*. When tempted to say *actual*, ask: do I mean "real" or "now-existing"? If the second → *current*.

#### 6. 🟡 Non-native verb-noun collocation — 2 instances

Some noun phrases don't pair with the verbs they pair with in RU/UA. Native speakers say *advise on*, not *do advisory on*. *Some sort of* takes a singular noun.

- "I'll also **do advisory on** AI-native PM workflows" → "I also **advise on** AI-native PM workflows" (or "do **advisory work on**")
- "**some sort of positive outcomes**" → "**some kind of positive outcome**" (singular after *some sort of / some kind of*)

**Drill:** When stuck, fall back to the simplest verb: *advise · review · analyse · build*. Avoid bolting *do/make* onto a noun unless you've heard it as a fixed phrase (*do research, make a decision* ✓ / *do advisory* ✗).

#### 7. 🔴 Wrong tech term — "hard-scripted" → "hard-coded" — 1 instance

In software, the term is **hard-coded** (values written directly into the source instead of read from config). *Hard-scripted* is not a term — to a developer-adjacent audience it reads as a mishearing or invented word, and it lands wrong in a PM-to-engineers context.

- "It might be **hard-scripted**, the key ones might be defined upfront and be part of that skill" → "It might be **hard-coded** — the key ones defined upfront as part of that skill"

**Drill:** *hard-coded values · hard-coded config · hard-coded list*. Never "hard-scripted".

#### 8. 🔴 Conditional with "will" after "if" — 1 instance (high-leverage rule)

In English conditionals, the *if*-clause takes the present tense even when describing the future. *If* + *will* is one of the single most recognisable non-native errors — worth fixing once and never repeating.

- "**If everything will work smoothly**, we will have a quick one iteration of live demo" → "**If everything works smoothly**, we'll do one quick iteration of a live demo"

**Drill:** *If* + present tense → *will* + base verb. *If it rains, I'll stay home.* Never *If it will rain, I'll stay home.*
