## Top items to study this session

#### 1. 🟡 Missing article on generic noun phrases — 6 instances

Spoken English drops articles when you're rushing, but generic singular count nouns ("environment", "matter", "role", "country") need one. The fix is mechanical: ask "is it 'a' (one of many) or 'the' (specific)?" before any singular count noun.

- "working in **enterprise environment**" → working in **an enterprise environment**
- "you are in **US**" → you are in **the US**
- "that's **technical matter**" → that's **a technical matter**
- "most of **product managers**" → most of **the product managers**
- "finding **the full-time role**" → finding **a full-time role** (one of many — indefinite)
- "have **the conversation** with the company" → have **a conversation** with the company

**Drill:** before any singular count noun in unscripted speech, run the "a vs. the vs. (zero)" check. Default to **a/an** for first-mention generics, **the** for ones you've already named.

#### 2. 🟡 "kind of / type of / sort of" + singular noun — 3 instances

These quantifiers govern a singular noun, never a plural. Plural needs *kinds of / types of*. Two instances of this in one call is a marker of L1 transfer.

- "this **kind of projects**" → this **kind of project** (or: **these kinds of projects**)
- "there's this **type of considerations**" → these **types of considerations** (also fixes the *there's*/plural agreement)
- "**small businesses users**" → **small-business users** (compound modifier stays singular)

**Drill:** *kind / type / sort* + **singular** noun. If the noun is plural, the quantifier must be plural too: *kinds / types / sorts of* + plural.

#### 3. 🟡 Wrong preposition in fixed verb patterns — 3 instances

A few specific verbs lock to specific prepositions in English. *Report* + person = **to** (never *into*). *Account* + thing = **for**. Get these wrong and the listener notices immediately.

- "the product manager who **reported into me**" → who **reported to me**
- "**reported into founders**" → **reported to the founders**
- "you need to **account that**" → you need to **account for that**

**Drill:** memorise these locked pairs — *report to* (someone), *account for* (something), *consist of*, *depend on*, *result in*. Drill them as units, not as verb + free preposition.

#### 4. 🟡 False friends / wrong-register single words — 4 instances

Four single-word slips where the meaning leaks through but the word is off. Each is a generalisable swap to internalise.

- "raised external capital quite **lately**" → quite **recently** (*lately* implies an ongoing recent state — "I've been tired lately"; for a single past event, use **recently**)
- "this **motion** kind of translates" → this **mentality** / this **approach** translates (*motion* means physical movement, not a way of operating)
- "I'm **pretty much sure**" → I'm **pretty sure** (*pretty much* = "almost"; combining it with *sure* is contradictory — drop *much*)
- "not **the essence**" → not **the substance** / not **what matters** (*essence* is philosophical/abstract; sounds odd in business register)

**Drill:** when reaching for *lately*, swap to **recently**. *Motion* → **approach / mentality**. *Pretty much sure* → **pretty sure**. *The essence* → **the substance** or **what matters**.

#### 5. 🟡 Run-on monologue — main point buried in a chain of clauses

The Creatio/Jooble explanation runs ~250 words with one main argument ("it's a dynamic environment") drowned in nested context. Interviewers stop tracking around clause 4. Native pacing: short topic sentence, then 1–2 supporting clauses, then full stop.

- The Creatio/Jooble culture passage (see §5 row 1 below for the rewrite)
- "It's definitely about the **quite speedy and quite dynamic environment**" → "It's a **fast, dynamic environment**" (one *quite* + one adjective is plenty)
- "So to sum it up, I'm pretty much sure that you should treat that as quite dynamic, honestly" → "**Bottom line: treat it as a very dynamic environment**"

**Drill:** in interview answers, after ~25 words look for the next natural full stop and *take it*. Then start a new sentence. Listener comprehension drops sharply past 3 clauses in one breath.
