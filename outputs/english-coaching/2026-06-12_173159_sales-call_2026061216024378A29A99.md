## Top items to study this session

#### 1. 🔴 Run-on / overlong sentence with broken structure — 4 instances

Strategic questions and wrap-up monologues are running 40–60 words across 3–5 clauses, and structure collapses partway: auxiliaries drop, articles vanish, the main point ends up in the last sliver. A listener has to re-parse to recover the ask.

- "And **yes, we just touched this a little bit of the Bellaroma Refire right now. I will know nothing this about that. They're completely on them, mostly on the Oracle suite, right? And we offering and they're asking for the AI-based use case, right? And there is exactly the— our benefit, USP on, on the having like mediator. And if you have data platform in the middle which enable them for the like data-driven use cases, that one of the USP, right?**" → *"Quick context on Bellaroma — they're mostly on the Oracle suite and asking us for AI-based use cases. That's exactly where a data platform in the middle is our USP: it enables the data-driven use cases on top of their existing stack."*
- "**do you see like stages of the approaching the maturity of the offerings? With kind of, I don't know, enabling the, enabling the credits for the user, for the clients to have, I know, to get to use this AI data platform.**" → *"Do you have a staged GTM as the offering matures — for example, free credits clients can use to try AIDP?"*
- "**how do you see the packaging, right, for the like different size of the clients and readiness of the clients as well to expand actually this consumption over the 1 million.**" → *"How do you see the packaging — for different client sizes and different readiness levels — to actually grow consumption past the one-million mark?"*

**Drill:** Before any strategic question, mentally state it in one short sentence: "What is the GTM by client size?" Then add ONE qualifier, then stop. If a third "like" or "kind of" is about to appear, the sentence is already over.

#### 2. 🟡 Missing article (a / the) — 7 instances

Article omission is the single most consistent RU/UA tell in the transcript. Especially before singular countable nouns introducing a concept: *a data platform*, *a mediator*, *an accelerator*, *a white paper*, *a common GTM*.

- "USP on, on the having like **mediator**" → USP of having **a mediator**
- "if you have **data platform** in the middle" → if you have **a data platform** in the middle
- "Is it also has **kind of accelerator**" → Does it also have **some kind of accelerator**
- "If we can also have some like **white paper** about this" → If we could also have **a white paper** on this
- "to think about kind of **common GTM**" → to think about **a common GTM**
- "when the client completely on **the Oracle**" → when the client is completely on **Oracle** (no article before company names)
- "**I can new Milo host**" → **I can let Milo host** (likely intended — verb missing too)

**Drill:** Quick mental check before any singular noun introducing a new thing: "one of these, or *the* specific one we already named?" If "one of these" → **a/an**. If neither feels right → it's a proper noun (Oracle, Databricks), drop the article.

#### 3. 🟡 Dropped or doubled auxiliary verb — 4 instances

The "to be" / "to have" auxiliary keeps getting skipped in present continuous, present perfect, and questions — or doubled by mistake.

- "And **we offering** and they're asking" → And **we're offering** and they're asking
- "I know **Gary is already left**" → I know **Gary has already left** (present perfect, not "is")
- "**Is it also has** kind of accelerator" → **Does it also have** some kind of accelerator (one verb, not two)
- "when the client **completely on the Oracle**" → when the client **is completely on** Oracle

**Drill:** Every sentence needs exactly one finite verb per clause. Before pressing send mentally, scan: "Where's my verb?" If it's a question about possession/capability → *Do you have …?* / *Does it have …?*, never *Is it has*.

#### 4. 🟡 Non-native verb-noun collocation — 3 instances

Verbs paired with the wrong particle or with a noun that doesn't take that verb in English.

- "Mustafa is **a bit pending on** this project" → Mustafa is **a bit stuck on** this project (or: *this project is a bit blocked on Mustafa*)
- "We wanted to **kick on this** much faster" → We wanted to **kick this off** much faster
- "We will **knock one more time** to move next week" → We'll **nudge them one more time** to move next week

**Drill:** Memorize the correct particle as part of the verb: *kick off*, *follow up*, *nudge*, *push*, *chase*. People and projects don't "pend" — they get **stuck**, **blocked**, or **delayed**.

#### 5. 🟡 "as well" used where "either" or nothing is needed — 1 instance (but high-leverage)

In negative sentences, "too / as well" must become **"either"**. This is a small rule with big surface area — it'll come up dozens of times a month.

- "You shouldn't rely on us **as well**" → You shouldn't rely on us **either**

**Drill:** Positive sentence → *too / as well*. Negative sentence → **either**. ("I like it too" / "I don't like it either.")

#### 6. 🟡 Conditional with "will" after "if" — 1 instance

In the *if*-clause of a real conditional, use the present tense, not *will*. *Will* belongs only in the result clause.

- "if you tell me **we will start** from Monday the DHL and NHS, I would be more than happy" → if you tell me **we'll start** DHL and NHS **on** Monday, I'd be more than happy *(present-tense "tell" is fine; the "will" in the if-clause itself is the issue — also reorder so "on Monday" doesn't dangle)*

**Drill:** *If X happens, Y will happen.* No "will" before the comma. "If you tell me **we start** Monday, I'll be happy" — never "if you will tell me".

#### 7. 🟡 Adverb placement (split infinitive in wrong spot) — 2 instances

Adverbs like *actually*, *probably*, *maybe* keep landing after the object rather than before the verb.

- "to **expand actually** this consumption" → to **actually expand** this consumption
- "I **would check** with Pavel **probably**" → I'd **probably check** with Pavel

**Drill:** Place *actually / probably / maybe* **right before the main verb**, not at the end of the sentence. "I'd probably check" / "to actually expand" — adverb hugs the verb.
