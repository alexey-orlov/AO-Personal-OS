## Executive summary

The heaviest load is in **§4 (grammar)** and **§1 (collocations)**, driven by two patterns: dropping **"the" before known referents** ("on Oracle side", "who is owner", "on pre-sales team") and **wrong prepositions in fixed collocations** ("invest into", "scale onto", "move forward productizing"). Both are classic Slavic-L1 carryovers and are high-leverage — a single rule each removes a long tail of small slips. The bottom line, though, is **comprehensibility (§5)**: in this discovery call your sentences repeatedly sprawl into 40–50-word chains with embedded clauses and either/or branches, and at least once the listener genuinely loses the thread. Fix the sprawl first — it costs you more in a clarity-seeking conversation than any single grammar slip. Severity totals: 🔴 1 · 🟡 35 · 🟢 7.

## Top items to study this session

#### 1. 🟡 Articles — missing "the" on known referents + spurious article on uncountables — 6 instances

Two opposite article errors. (a) When you point at a *specific, already-known* thing (a named team, a side of the deal), English wants **the**. (b) Abstract/uncountable nouns (*clarity*, *scope*) take **no** article. RU/UA has no articles, so both directions need conscious checking.

- "on **Ø** Oracle side" → "on **the** Oracle side"
- "who is **Ø** owner" → "who is **the** owner"
- "get **the** full clarity" → "get **Ø** full clarity"

**Drill:** Two-question check on every noun: *specific and known?* → add **the**. *Abstract mass noun (clarity / scope / feedback)?* → drop the article.

#### 2. 🟡 Wrong / missing preposition in collocations — 4 instances

Verbs lock to specific prepositions in English, and they rarely match the Russian one. These are memorised per-verb, not derived.

- "**invest into** something" → "**invest in** something"
- "**scaling** that **onto** the customers" → "**scaling** that **to** the customers"
- "**move forward** productizing" → "**move forward with** productizing"

**Drill:** Lock the set: *invest **in** · scale **to** · move forward **with** · vision **for** · engage **with***.

#### 3. 🔴 One over-long sentence that loses the listener — 1 instance

A grammatical sentence can still fail if it runs 40+ words through two embedded clauses and an either/or. In a discovery call where you're asking *them* for clarity, your own question has to be parseable in one pass.

- "Are they really willing to move forward productizing the solutions that does not utilize their agentic platform, just continue working with NVIDIA's one, or it's just a temporary approach, and the ultimate goal is to build the solutions that utilizes their analogous platform." → "**Do they want to keep building on NVIDIA's platform long-term, or is that just temporary until they move everything onto their own OCI agentic platform?**"

**Drill:** One sentence = one question. When you hear yourself say "or it's just… and the ultimate goal is…", stop and split it into two short questions.

#### 4. 🟡 Non-native collocation: "speak the same terms" — 2 instances

The English idiom is "use the same terms" or "speak the same **language**". "Speak the same terms" is a direct calque.

- "to **speak the same terms**" → "to **use the same terms**"
- "do we **speak the same terms**?" → "are we **using the same terms**?"

**Drill:** *speak the same **language** · use the same **terms***. Never "speak terms".

#### 5. 🟡 Negation placement: "might be not" → "might not be" — 2 instances

The negator attaches to the modal/auxiliary, not after the verb: **might not be**, not "might be not".

- "we **might be not** in that good a position" → "we **might not be** in that good a position"
- "it **might be not** the topic to handle now" → "it **might not be** the topic to handle now"

**Drill:** *not* goes right after the modal: *might **not** be · would **not** be · can**not** be*.

#### 6. 🟡 Subject–verb agreement with "solutions that…" — 2 instances

A relative clause agrees with the plural head noun: *solutions that **do** / that **utilize***, not "does / utilizes".

- "solutions that **does** not utilize" → "solutions that **do** not utilize"
- "solutions that **utilizes**" → "solutions that **utilize**"

**Drill:** Find the noun the "that" refers back to. Plural noun → bare plural verb (*do, utilize, have*).

#### 7. 🟡 Calque phrasings: "exposed to" / "goal is in front of me" — 2 instances

Direct translations from Russian that land as odd in English. Replace with the native equivalent.

- "I'm already **exposed to** product management team" → "I've already **been introduced to** the product team"
- "same goal **is in front of me**" → "**I share the same goal**"

**Drill:** When a phrase feels translated, test the plain verb: *introduced to · I have the same goal*.

#### 8. 🟡 Indirect-question word order — 1 instance

After "tell you / know / understand", the embedded question keeps **statement** order — no inversion.

- "I can tell you **what was my goal**" → "I can tell you **what my goal was**"

**Drill:** Direct: *What was my goal?* Embedded: *…what my goal **was**.* Subject before verb.
