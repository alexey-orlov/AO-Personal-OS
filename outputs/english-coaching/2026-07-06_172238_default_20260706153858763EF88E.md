## Top items to study this session

#### 1. 🟡 "related to" for involvement / ownership — 2 instances

In English a person is *involved in*, *responsible for*, or *working on* something — not "related to" it. "Related to" links two things by topic; it doesn't connect a person to their job (RU *относиться к* misfires here).

- "the part that I **was related to**" → the part I **was involved in**
- "I am kind of **related to** the product thing" → I'm kind of **responsible for** the product side

**Drill:** person + work → *involved in / responsible for / working on*. Reserve "related to" for thing-to-thing ("this issue is related to staffing").

#### 2. 🟡 Trim redundancy & untangle long sentences — 3 instances

The biggest drag on clarity: doubled words and sentences carrying three clauses on one breath. A listener loses the thread.

- "our **recent** engagement with Oracle that we **recently** signed" → our **recent** Oracle engagement
- "…a win **would be** worth mentioning **as a win**" → …would be worth mentioning
- "It's our distance that we keep from the customers and the moments when the most intense … conversation … happens, which prevents us from generating insights about what we can build, package, do next." → "The distance we keep from customers — at exactly the moments the richest conversations happen — stops us generating insight into what to build next."

**Drill:** after "recent/recently, actual/actually, win," check you didn't say it twice. One idea → one sentence; if a sentence has three "and"s, cut it in two.

#### 3. 🟡 Non-native collocations & set phrases — 3 instances

Small word-choice slips that read as non-native even though the grammar is fine.

- "accelerate our **competencies**" → **build / expand** our competencies
- "achieve **in some time**" → achieve **eventually** / **given enough time**
- "do I **get it correctly** that…" → do I **understand correctly** that… / do I **have this right**

**Drill:** you *accelerate* growth/pace, you *build* competencies. "In some time" is a RU calque (*со временем*) — say *eventually* or *over time*.

#### 4. 🟢 Finish the clause before restarting — 2 instances

Twice the user abandoned a clause that was already landing cleanly and restarted from scratch, which reads as more hesitant than the English actually was.

- "A frustration is probably **our**— It's my observation. It's our distance…" → "A frustration is probably **our distance from the customers**."

**Drill:** when a sentence is 80% out and grammatical, push it to the period instead of resetting — the reset costs more clarity than the imperfect ending would.
