# 1) Grammar to learn

Eight rules. Drilled in priority order — each one targets the highest-volume pattern still left.

### 1.1 🔴 Articles on singular countable nouns — THE rule

This is the heaviest single pattern in your English and the fastest "non-native" signal a native ear picks up. Every singular countable noun in English needs an article (or another determiner like *my/this/each*). RU/UA has zero articles, so the default L1 instinct is to drop them. You must override that instinct mechanically until it becomes automatic.

**The decision tree** (run this on every singular countable noun you say):

1. **Is it countable?** If you can say "one X / two Xs", yes. (Examples: *meeting, model, presence, approach, habit, priority, POC, LLM, CRM, framework, role, opportunity*.) If no — it's uncountable (*feedback, advice, detail, information, research, software, equipment, progress, work*) — skip articles entirely; use *the* only when specific.
2. **If countable: is it specific (we both know which one)?** → **the**.
3. **Otherwise** → **a / an**.

**High-frequency slots where you systematically drop the article** (these come up in every call):

- After *with*, *as*, *into*, *from*, *like*, *under* → "with **a** fintech startup", "as **a** product leader", "under **the** assumption".
- Before *type/kind/sort of* + singular → "**an** entrepreneurial type of thing", "**a** different sort of platform".
- Before adjective + role/title → "**a** Boston-based SaaS company", "**a** good number".
- Country names that take the definite article: **the** UK, **the** Netherlands, **the** US, **the** Philippines, **the** Czech Republic.
- Brand/tool names: **Excel** has no article. **Miro** is used with *on* (*on Miro*), not "*in the Miro*".

**Set phrases where the article is fixed (memorise as chunks):**

| Phrase | Article |
|---|---|
| under **the** assumption | the |
| in **a** vacuum | a |
| go **the** extra mile | the |
| set **the** stage | the |
| take **the** lead | the |
| at **the** end of **the** day | the … the |
| on **the** same page | the |
| **the** task at hand | the |
| **the** source of truth | the |
| **the** bottom line | the |
| in **the** long run | the |
| in **the** background | the |
| in **an** ideal world | an |
| in **the** near future | the |

**Drill (do this for one week):** before pressing send on any written sentence (Slack, email), scan every noun. Insert an article anywhere a singular countable noun is bare. The reflex transfers to speech in ~2 weeks of daily practice.

### 1.2 🔴 Conditionals — never `will/'ll` after `if`

Slipped in **at least 7 separate places** across the calls. This is a binary rule with zero exceptions in standard conditional clauses, so it's high-leverage.

- **Real / future condition:** `if + present, will + base verb`
  *"If it **rains**, we **will** stay home."* — never *"if it will rain"*.
- **Hypothetical / present-unreal:** `if + past simple, would + base`
  *"If I **had** more time, I **would** do it."*
- **Past-unreal:** `if + past perfect, would have + past participle`
  *"If I **had done** that on my own, it **would have been** less than 40 slides."*

Examples you produced (across the 5 calls):

- "if **you'll be working** on something" → "if **you're working**"
- "if **you'll see** something" → "if **you see** something"
- "if **you'll have** one minute more" → "if **you have** one minute more"
- "if **we'll have** enough time" → "if **we have** enough time"
- "if **everything will work** smoothly" → "if **everything works**"
- "**If we will be able** to deliver" → "**If we are able** to deliver"
- "**If I was doing** that on my own, **it's going to be** less than 40 slides" → "**If I had done** that on my own, **it would have been** less"

**Mental check:** every time the word *if* leaves your mouth, the next verb must NOT be *will/'ll*. If it is — strip it. The future meaning lives in the main clause only.

### 1.3 🟡 Indirect questions — strip the inversion

A direct question inverts subject and verb: *"What **was** the conversation?"*. Once the question is embedded inside another clause, you **undo the inversion**: *"…recap what the conversation **was**"*.

This pattern leaked in **all 5 reports**, ~10 total instances. The fix is mechanical: write the direct question first, then drop the inversion when you embed it.

| Direct (inverted) | Embedded (statement order) |
|---|---|
| What **are the channels**? | …understand what **the channels are**. |
| How **big is it**? | …I don't know how **big it is**. |
| What **do they do** next? | …the agents decide what **they do** next. |
| What **was** the conversation? | …recap what **the conversation was**. |
| What **is the next use case**? | …see what **the next use case is**. |
| What **is the real condition**? | …match it with what **the real condition is**. |

Triggers that signal an embedded question coming: *understand · see · know · ask · decide · figure out · let's see · I wonder · I'm not sure · explain · show me*.

Bonus pattern: "**how it looks like**" is wrong twice over. Pick one:
- "**what** it looks like" (idiomatic, recommended)
- "**how** it looks" (also fine)

Never combine them.

### 1.4 🟡 Countable vs. uncountable — much/many, little/few, no plural -s on uncountables

A short, closed list of common-but-tricky uncountables that you systematically pluralise or quantify wrongly:

| Uncountable noun | Wrong | Right |
|---|---|---|
| feedback | *those feedbacks · much of feedback* | **that feedback · much feedback** |
| advice | *advices · much of advice* | **advice · much advice / a piece of advice** |
| information | *informations* | **information** |
| detail | *level of details · those details* (when meaning *amount of detail*) | **level of detail · much detail** |
| research | *researches* | **research / studies** |
| evidence | *evidences* | **evidence** |
| software | *softwares · a software* | **software / a piece of software** |
| equipment | *equipments* | **equipment** |
| progress | *progresses* | **progress** |
| work | *works* (when meaning labour) | **work** |
| room (= space) | *few room* | **little room** |
| turnover (= churn) | *high turnovers* | **high turnover** |
| logging | *how much of logging* | **how much logging** |
| fruit (idiom) | *low-hanging fruits* | **low-hanging fruit** |

**Quantifier rule:**

- Countables → **few / many / a number of**: *many conversations, few meetings, a few roadblocks*.
- Uncountables → **little / much / a lot of / a great deal of**: *little time, much information, a lot of feedback*.
- Safe in either case: **a lot of, plenty of, some**.

**Test:** if you can naturally say *"a piece of X"* — X is uncountable. No plural, no *many*, no *few*.

### 1.5 🟡 Adverb placement — `aux + ADV + verb`

Adverbs like *definitely, probably, also, already, fully, actually, in fact* go **between the first auxiliary and the main verb**, not before the auxiliary, not after the verb's object, and not between *going to* and the verb.

| Wrong | Right |
|---|---|
| it **fully is** based on… | it **is fully** based on… |
| are they **interested actually** in… | are they **actually interested** in… |
| it should be **also added**… | it should **also be added**… |
| there will be **definitely** the deviation | there **will definitely be** some deviation |
| it's going to be **definitely** less than 40 slides | it's **definitely going to be** less than 40 slides |
| you want **probably** be able to… | you **probably want** to be able to… |
| I **already was** able to deliver… | I **was already** able to deliver… |
| I **also was** thinking about that | I **was also** thinking about that |
| human will be **always** in the loop | the human will **always be** in the loop |
| keeping it **always** up to date | **always** keeping it up to date |
| it doesn't influence **anymore** the supply | it **no longer** influences the supply / doesn't influence the supply **anymore** |

**Default slot:** *aux + ADV + verb*. *"She **has always** liked it." · "It **will definitely** work." · "They **are also** considering."*

Note: `anymore / any longer` are different — they always go at the very end of the clause.

### 1.6 🟡 Polite `would` with *appreciate / like / love / prefer*

These verbs take **would/'d**, never **will/'ll**, in polite-request frames. Saying *"I'll appreciate"* sounds like a strange promise about your future emotions, not a polite request.

Fixed politeness frames:

- **I'd appreciate it if** you could…
- **I'd like to** ask you about…
- **I'd love to** hear more about…
- **I'd prefer** to do it this way.
- **It would be great if**…

Slipped twice in your transcripts: *"I'll appreciate maybe if you can reiterate"* → *"I'd appreciate it if you could recap"* · *"I will appreciate any comments"* → *"I'd appreciate any comments"*.

---

### 1.7 🟡 `help + bare infinitive` (no -ing, no `to`)

After **help (someone)**, the next verb is the bare infinitive:

- "help us **accelerate**" ✓ (not *help us accelerating*, not *help us to accelerate*)
- "help us **ship**, **decide**, **grow**, **figure out**" ✓

This came up in the Kat call. Same family: **let, make** also take bare infinitives. *"Let me know"* (not *to know*); *"It made me realise"* (not *to realise*).

### 1.8 🟡 Past-context tense discipline & present perfect

Two recurring slips:

**(a) Past context → past tense throughout.** When narrating something that happened, don't drop into present tense mid-story.

- "It was super exciting in terms of **it doesn't feel** like" → "**it didn't feel** like"
- "Then when **we have** something that is showing signs of success, then **we invest**" → "**when we had** something showing signs of success, **we'd invest**"
- "We decided deliberately, **we don't have capacity**" → "**we didn't have capacity**"

**(b) Present perfect for life-experience and "up to now" claims.** Use **have/has + past participle** when the time frame extends into the present, or for completed actions whose result/relevance is now.

- "those who **never worked** with that" → "those who **have never worked** with that"
- "the role **evolved already** during this year" → "the role **has already evolved** this year"
- "**do you define** some areas for yourself where you need to grow?" → "**have you defined** some areas for yourself where you need to grow?"

**(c) Stative verbs don't take continuous aspect.** *Realise, know, believe, understand, want, need, like, mean, see (= understand)* — use simple present, not present continuous.

- "they **are not even realizing** they are part of that process" → "they **don't even realize**"

# 2) Idioms & vocabulary to learn

### 2.1 🟡 RU/UA false friends — the systematic deletion list

Words that look like an English cognate but mean something different (or aren't used the same way). These appeared in multiple calls — you'll keep producing them until you actively block the L1 reflex.

| You said (calque) | Correct English | Note |
|---|---|---|
| **actual** stack / **actual** capabilities | **current** stack / **current** capabilities | *Actual* in EN = "real, true" (opposite of *fake*), not "current". |
| **classical** ML / **classical** ERP / **classical** meaning | **classic** ML / **classic** ERP / **classic** sense | *Classical* = of antiquity (Mozart, Aristotle). Anything else → *classic*. |
| **economical** terms | **economic** terms | *Economical* = thrifty (saves money). *Economic* = relating to the economy. |
| **absolutely** different | **completely** different | *Absolutely* intensifies categorical adjectives (*absolutely right/wrong*), not gradable ones. |
| in the **nearest** future | in the **near** future | *Nearest* = closest in distance/order; *near* = approaching. |
| **outstanding** one (= remaining/exceptional) | **glaring** exception / **standout** issue | *Outstanding* in EN almost always = excellent. |
| **personas** they see fit (= candidates) | **type of person** they're looking for | *Persona* = UX/marketing archetype, not a real candidate. |
| **on the contrary**, X | **on the flip side / conversely**, X | *On the contrary* directly contradicts a prior claim; you usually mean "or the opposite case". |
| **from one side** … **on the other side** | **on the one hand** … **on the other hand** | Fixed pair. |
| **as I know** | **as far as I know** | The hedge needs *far as*. |
| in the **meanwhile** | in the **meantime** | Frozen form. |

### 2.3 🟡 Verb-preposition pairings — memorise as units, not as verb + preposition

English verbs and prepositions are "married". You can't translate the preposition from RU/UA. Most of your slips fall on the same ~15 verbs.

**Verbs that take ON:**
- **elaborate on** a topic
- **build on** an idea
- **reflect on** something
- **touch on** a topic *(touched **on** that, not "touched that")*
- **expand on** an idea
- **focus on** X *(not "focus of/in X")*
- **double down on** an approach
- **push back on** an idea
- **work on** something
- **comment on** X

**Verbs that take TO:**
- **subject to** X *(not "subject for")*
- **reach out to** someone *(not "reach to")*
- **listen to** X
- **respond to** X
- **belong to** X
- **react to** X
- **apply to** X *(a request that doesn't apply to other cases)*

**Verbs that take AT:**
- **take a look at** X *(not "take a look on")*
- **look at** X *(not "look on")*
- **arrive at** a decision/conclusion
- **at different times** *(not "in different times")*
- **on the surface** *(not "at the surface")* — counter-example, watch for this

**Verbs that take FOR:**
- **compensate for** X *(not "compensate X")*
- **account for** X
- **opt for** X
- **wait for** X *(not "wait X")*
- **search for** X / **look for** X *(but: "seek X" — transitive, no *for*)*

**Verbs that take IN:**
- **career in** X *(not "career with")*
- **move in** a direction *(not "into")*
- **interested in** X *(not "interested of/to")*
- **specialise in** X
- **succeed in** X-ing

**Verbs that take WITH or ABOUT:**
- **empathy with / for** X *(not "empathy to")*
- **frustrated with** X
- **feel about** X *(not "feel with")*
- **think about** X
- **wonder about** X
- **remind X about Y** *(needs both objects — "remind me about the form", not "remind about the form")*

**Verbs that take NO preposition (transitive):**
- **seek** X *(no "for")*
- **trust** X *(no "in")* *(though "trust in" is OK for abstract nouns: "trust in the system")*
- **reiterate** X *(no "on")*
- **recap** X *(no "on")*
- **lack** X
- **discuss** X *(no "about")*

**Verb particles — `look on/at/up`, `take out/up/over`, `push back/away`:**
- **take action** (no article — *"or take action right away"*)
- **make an effort** (not *do an effort*)
- **make a decision** (not *take/do a decision*)
- **make a case** (not *do a case*)
- **go on vacation** (not *for*, not *to*)
- **roll X out** / **pull X up** — when the object is a pronoun or short noun, the particle moves to the end: *"roll **this** out"*, *"pull **that backlog item** up"*

**Drill method:** when you encounter a verb in reading or listening, capture the *full unit* (verb + preposition + sample object), not the verb alone. Anki cards as "verb + preposition + 1 example", not "verb = translation".

# 3) Other things to do

Behaviours and habits — not strict grammar/vocab — that account for the most damaging errors (🔴) in your transcripts.

### 3.1 🔴 Shorten sentences. Plant the period earlier.

Almost every 🔴 sentence-collapse in your transcripts happened in a sentence past ~20 words, where two embedded clauses (*"…what might be the ideas for the strategy look like"*) tangled. The fix is structural:

- **Hard rule: if a sentence has more than one `that` / `what` / `which`, stop and split it.**
- **Soft rule:** once you start a *because* / *which is* clause, mentally count to ~15 words and end the sentence. Start fresh.

Practice with the "**short-sentence rewrite**" exercise: take the long sentence from your transcript, split it into 2–3 sentences each containing at most one clause. After 2 weeks, the parsing pressure drops.

### 3.2 🔴 Finish the noun phrase before adding qualifiers

You repeatedly tried to insert a qualifier *inside* a noun phrase mid-flow (*"transform my if I'm a PM work"*). English requires you to complete the noun first, then append the qualifier as a separate clause.

- ❌ "transform **my if I'm a PM work**"
- ✅ "transform **my work as a PM**"
- ✅ "transform **a PM's work**"

If you reach for a noun and the qualifier doesn't fit cleanly: finish with a placeholder ("**…transform the PM's work**"), then add the qualifier afterwards as a new clause if needed ("**…specifically when you're early-career**").

### 3.3 🟡 Cut one hedge per sentence

You sometimes stack two hedges that cancel each other or simply over-soften:

- "all the content is targeted **specifically** to product managers **mainly**" — pick one.
- "It's **kind of definitely** a formative experience" — pick *definitely*.
- "**Maybe** it **might** be just the full copy" — pick *maybe* OR *might*, not both.
- "**quite a very** dense agenda" — pick *quite a* OR *very*.
- "**not that super critical**" — pick *not that* OR *not super*.

**Rule:** one qualifier per claim. If the front of the sentence already has *specifically / exactly / only / definitely*, the back doesn't need *mainly / mostly / kind of*.

### 3.4 🟡 No double negatives — restart if you tangle

"please **don't hesitate to not postpone** them" — the listener parses backwards and gives up. If you catch yourself stacking negatives, stop and restart with a clean structure.

- Target: max one negative per clause.
- If you need to express both, split: *"Please raise them as you go. Don't save them for the end."*

### 3.5 🟡 Cut redundant synonym pairs

You stack synonyms that add nothing:

- ❌ "**highlighted and mentioned**" — pick *highlighted*.
- ❌ "**previous history**" — *history*.
- ❌ "**common consensus**" — *consensus*.
- ❌ "**core essential** information" — *essential information*.
- ❌ "**returned back**" — *returned*.
- ❌ "**wider and broader**" — *broader*.
- ❌ "**multiple and multiple times**" — *many times*.

Mental check: if two words next to each other mean the same thing, delete one.

### 3.6 🟡 Pre-meeting / pre-call drills

Before a high-stakes call (interview, demo, customer call), run a **5-minute warm-up** with this checklist out loud:

1. Say five conditional sentences out loud: *"If you have a moment, I'd like to walk you through…"*, *"If we see X, we'll do Y."*, *"If I had more time, I would…"* — train the no-*will*-after-*if* reflex.
2. Say five embedded-question sentences: *"I'm curious what the key drivers are."*, *"Let me understand how the team is organised."*, *"I'd like to know what the timeline looks like."*
3. Recite the polite frames: *"I'd appreciate it if…"*, *"Would you mind if…"*, *"Could you walk me through…"*.
4. Speak one paragraph of "today I want to cover…" with deliberate articles before every singular countable noun.

5 minutes, every call. The patterns transfer because they're the same ~5 grammar rules.

### 3.7 🟡 Post-call habit: harvest 3–5 phrases

After every call you record, before the day ends:

1. Scan the transcript for the **3 best native phrases the other person used**. Add them to a "phrases to steal" file.
2. Scan your own speech for the **2 worst slips**. Drill the corrected versions out loud 3 times each.

This is more efficient than full grammar review because it's anchored to actual usage in your domain.

### 3.8 🟡 Listen and shadow native B2B product / VC content

Anki and grammar drills handle the *rule* level. For the *collocation* level — *operating model*, *pain points*, *go-to-market*, *step-change*, *flywheel*, *land and expand*, *commercial motion*, *signal strength*, *table stakes* — you need to hear them in context. Highest-leverage sources for your domain:

- **Lenny's Podcast** — exact register (B2B SaaS PM / CPO interviews).
- **Lenny + Friends Summit talks** on YouTube.
- **20VC (Harry Stebbings)** — VC + CPO conversations.
- **First Round Review** articles (read aloud sometimes — train the rhythm).
- **a16z podcast** (enterprise episodes).

**Shadowing exercise:** play a 1-minute clip, pause every sentence, repeat it out loud trying to match cadence and stress. 10 minutes daily compounds fast.

### 3.9 🟡 Track progress with the same skill

Re-run [english-coaching](/.claude/skills/english-coaching/) on every recorded call. After ~10 reports:

- The same patterns recurring = the drill isn't sticking → switch drill method (write instead of speak, or vice versa).
- New patterns appearing = your speech got more ambitious → that's progress, not regression.

Single metric to watch over the next 10 calls: **🔴 count per call**. Targets: drop to ≤3 within 4 weeks, ≤1 within 12 weeks. The 🟡 count drops naturally as the article + conditional + indirect-question rules become automatic.
